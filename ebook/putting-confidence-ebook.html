Introduction

Why Most Golfers Never Improve Their Putting

Most golfers wonder why they struggle with putting. The stroke itself is deceptively simple — a one-lever motion that any physically capable person can execute. There are thousands of putters, thousands of techniques, endless YouTube tips. And yet putting remains the single greatest source of frustration on the golf course.

The reason isn't physical. It's structural. The real culprit is the invisible process every golfer goes through on every single putt — whether they realize it or not. Most golfers have never named that process. They've never broken it into steps. They have no idea which step breaks down when the putts stop falling.

"Defining the process we go through — whether we are aware of it or not — is the most important aspect of putting, yet we never talk about it or evaluate it."

- Mike Schy, PGA Professional

That process is the Ten Universal Putting Segments. Every golfer — from the weekend hacker to the Tour professional — goes through these ten steps on every putt. The difference between great putters and everyone else is simple: great putters have developed each segment until it is completely second nature. They move unconsciously from one segment to the next, with total freedom and total confidence.

This book will give you that same system. You will understand what each segment is, why it matters, how to practice it in isolation, and how to detect exactly where your process breaks down during a round. No more vague frustration. No more "I just couldn't putt today." You will know precisely what happened and precisely how to fix it.

The Promise

After studying and practicing the Ten Segments, you will be able to approach any putt — anywhere, at any time — with complete confidence. Not because you will make every putt, but because you will own the process that gives you the best possible chance on every single one.

How to Use This Book

Read through each segment once, end to end, to understand the complete picture. Then return to each segment and practice it in isolation on the practice green before integrating it into your full routine. The goal is not to make putting more complicated — it's to make your current unconscious process deliberate, so you can develop and trust it completely.

A companion Workbook and Quick Reference Card are available at GolfGenii.com to help you track your progress and carry the system on the course.

1
Segment One
Observation
The Green Read — The Macro View
15 - 20 Seconds

Before you ever reach your ball, the green is already communicating with you. In Segment 1, you are gathering the big picture — the overall slope, the high and low points, the drainage routes, the texture, the speed. Most golfers walk directly to their ball and mark it, completely skipping this segment. This is one of the most costly mistakes in putting.

Why It Matters

Your brain is continuously receiving both visual and kinesthetic information as you walk onto a green. The slopes you feel with your feet, the firmness of the surface, the shade patterns — all of this feeds your subconscious processing. When you pay conscious attention to this data, you dramatically accelerate and improve your putt read in Segment 2.

Your Observation Checklist

  • Note the overall slope of the green — where are the high and low points?
  • Observe where water would naturally drain off the green
  • Locate the drains and exits relative to the hole position
  • Notice whether the green is in shade or direct sunlight
  • Sense the firmness of the surface with your feet as you walk on
  • Assess whether the green has been mowed or rolled today
  • Check if the flag is positioned near an exit or slope change
  • Enter the green from the front when possible — your angle of entry changes your perception
Key Insight

Many optical illusions exist on golf greens that will mislead you unless you understand the full picture first. Greens that look flat from one angle can have significant slope from another. Building the macro view first protects you from these illusions in the micro read.

2
Segment Two
The Putt Read
The Micro View — The Vector Method
10 - 15 Seconds

A putt read is fundamentally different from the green read in Segment 1. Where Segment 1 was about the whole canvas, Segment 2 is about the specific putt in front of you — its exact break, the slope at the hole, and the precise aim point you will commit to.

Mike Schy uses the Vector Green Reading system developed by Col. H.A. Templeton. It provides a clear, precise, step-by-step process for reading any putt — single or multi-breaking — with repeatable confidence.

The Vector Green Reading — 7 Step Process

  1. Find the straight putt above the hole. (The Gravity Vector "arrow" lies on this line — its point is over the center of the cup, its other end is the aim point.)
  2. Estimate the slope at the hole, or use a contour map if available.
  3. Pace off the length of your putt.
  4. Go to the pages in your Vector Green Book that match today's green speed.
  5. Find the column headed by your putt's length.
  6. The big black number in that box is your aim point distance above the hole.
  7. Plot that aim point above the hole. This is the target you will aim your ball at.
Practice Note

Practicing your putt read is just as important as practicing your actual stroke — yet most golfers never do it. The better you become as a putt reader, the more naturally and confidently you will move through all the remaining segments. Learning to read putts is a skill that compounds over time.

3
Segment Three
Determining the Aim Point
Commit to Your Read — No Second-Guessing
5 Seconds

Contrary to popular belief, determining your aim point is not the same as reading the putt. In this segment, the reading is done. You are not re-evaluating your read — you are locking in your aim point and committing to it. This segment exists specifically because doubt tends to creep in between the read and the execution.

If you feel uncertain, the correct response is to return to Segment 2 and re-read. But if you are confident in your number, move forward immediately. The whole process moves quickly — stepping back momentarily is never a problem. Hesitating without moving back is where golfers lose putts.

The Rule

No second-guessing in Segment 3. You are either confident in your aim point and moving to Segment 4, or you are stepping back to Segment 2 for a cleaner read. There is no third option.

Segment 4: Aiming the Ball

5 - 10 Seconds

Once you have committed to your aim point, the next task is aiming your golf ball precisely at it. Research shows that only 17% of golfers consistently aim their ball correctly from 15 feet — and that number drops sharply at greater distances.

The Process

  1. Place your ball marker on the green behind the ball.
  2. Aim the marker directly at your aim point.
  3. Place the ball and align your line(s) so they point at the aim point.
  4. Verify both lines are aimed correctly, then remove the marker.
Critical Point

Once you have aimed your ball, there is no room for doubt. The ball is aimed. The line doesn't lie. Trust it completely as you move through the remaining segments.

5
Segment Five
Starting the Putt Routine
Commitment, Breathing, and Rhythm
3 - 5 Seconds

People often mistake the entire Ten Segment process for a putting routine. This segment, specifically, is the moment where you mentally and physically commit to executing the putt. It is a brief but essential transition — from preparation to action.

The elements of Segment 5: breathe correctly (a slow exhale releases tension), become aware of your natural rhythm, and consciously trust everything you've done in Segments 1 through 4. Only when you feel genuinely committed should you move to Segment 6.

What Commitment Feels Like

Commitment is not the absence of thought — it's the absence of doubt. A committed golfer isn't forcing confidence; they have earned it by completing each prior segment correctly. If commitment feels forced, something earlier in the process was incomplete.

Segment 6: Determining Speed

10 - 15 Seconds

Speed is the velocity of the putter at impact that delivers the ball to the hole at the correct terminal velocity — no more than one foot past the hole if you miss. Most golfers struggle with speed because they failed to fully commit to the aim point in Segment 3, which causes their attention to drift during speed determination.

4 Proven Speed Drills

Drill 1
The Release Drill
Take the putter back too far, hold. Then bring it to the spot you believe is perfect for the correct speed and let it fall. Stroke with zero tension. Evaluate and repeat — you'll quickly find where grip pressure and tension live.
Drill 2
The Yardstick Drill
Tape a metal yardstick with colored tape every two inches. Take the putter back 4 inches and swing through 4 inches. Note how far the ball travels. Repeat at each 2-inch interval up the yardstick. Contact with the ball should feel heavy, not sharp.
Drill 3
Metronome Drill
Repeat Drill 2 with a metronome. Start at 76 bpm and vary the tempo. Observe how tempo changes affect distance. Find your natural, comfortable beat.
Drill 4
Large Target Drill
Putt to a large object (not a hole), focusing only on speed from various distances and slopes. Step 1: Look at target. Step 2: Estimate stroke length and practice. Step 3: Step in, do not look at target again. Make stroke. Step 4: Evaluate distance control.
7
Segment Seven
Aiming the Putter
The A Effect, E Effect, and How to Beat Them
3 - 8 Seconds

Your goal is to step into the putt with confidence and repeatable rhythm and align the line on your putter with the line on your ball. Simple in concept — but this is where over 90% of golfers introduce doubt without realizing why.

When you read the putt and determined speed from behind the ball, your eyes were horizontal — level with the ground. When you stand over the putt, your head tilts and your eyes are no longer on the same plane relative to the target. This perceptual shift is what Col. Templeton called the "A effect" and "E effect."

The A / E Effect

Looking at an object horizontally and then looking at it vertically changes your perception. The putt that looked like it broke 6 inches from behind the ball now looks different from over it. Most golfers react to this by second-guessing their read and adjusting — which destroys the work done in Segments 1-6. The solution: do not look at the hole from over the ball. Trust the line. Aim the putter at the ball line. Pull the trigger.

The Aiming Sequence

  1. Get behind the ball looking directly down your line to your aim point.
  2. Take several practice strokes that you believe deliver the ball at the proper speed.
  3. Choose method A (look at target while making practice strokes) or method B (look at target, then look down at putter head and make stroke).
  4. Step into the putt precisely and rhythmically. Do not look at the hole.
  5. Align the line on your putter with the line on your ball. That's it.

Segment 8: Making the Stroke

2 - 3 Seconds

Only make the stroke when you are completely confident that you have completed all prior segments. There should be no clutter in your mind. The putter is lined up. You are in your stance. Make the stroke.

The correct stroke arc for you is determined by two factors: your forward bend (shoulder plane to ground) and your hand position relative to vertical. There are six arc types — square to square through 65 degrees — and none is universally superior. The right stroke is the one that is repeatable for you at every angle and distance, requiring no manipulation. Regardless of arc type, the putter face must always be square to its path.

Putter Fitting Note

An incorrectly fitted putter can appear to be lined up when the face is actually pointing left or right of the target due to improper lie angle. A correct putter fit — matching toe hang to your arc, proper lie angle, correct length, and an aiming line you can align — removes a significant variable from your stroke.

Post-Putt Evaluation and Self-Assessment

Segment 9: Post-Putt Evaluation

Your post-putt evaluation should be simple, fast, and emotionally neutral. Ask three quick questions:

  • How was the stroke? Did I repeat my practice stroke, or did I panic and change it?
  • How accurate was my speed — where did the ball finish relative to the hole?
  • How accurate was the read — was I on the right break and right page in my Vector Green Book?

During a round, evaluate only distance and line from Segment 9. Save stroke mechanics analysis for after the round, unless there is a complete breakdown that requires immediate attention. Small adjustments to your read or aim point for subsequent putts are the primary output of this segment.

When to Dig Deeper

Pushes, pulls, hits, and deceleration should be analyzed after the round — not while competing. The one exception: if your stroke completely falls apart, a brief mechanics check is warranted. Otherwise, trust your preparation.

Segment 10: Self-Assessment

Every golfer performs a self-assessment after a putt. The problem is that for most golfers, it's negative and confusing — because they have no framework for understanding what went wrong. When you don't know which segment failed, your self-assessment defaults to "I'm a bad putter." That belief compounds every round.

With the Ten Segments, your self-assessment becomes precise and constructive. Ask yourself: "Did I complete each segment? Where did I get stuck?" If you can identify the specific segment that broke down, you can fix it. That's the entire point.

"Becoming a great putter starts by saying 'I'm a great putter' and believing it. Great putters never allow negative thoughts to dominate their self-assessments."

- Mike Schy

Great putters on Tour hit 13-15 greens per round and may make only 5-9 first putts. They finish 28-30 putts. They don't make miracles — they manage themselves, read greens accurately, and give themselves the easiest possible putts to make. That is entirely learnable. And it starts with what you say to yourself after every putt.

Say this: "I am a great putter." Then identify what to improve. Move on.

Practice Plan

Always begin practice with a straight putt from 5 feet above the hole, then from 5 feet below the hole. Evaluate for any pushes or pulls. Gradually move to 15 feet, keeping the putt straight.

Drill: String Line
Uphill and Downhill Speed Control
Putt to a string line. First uphill, then downhill. Start at 3 feet, gradually move as far as possible. Goal: finish 6-12 inches past the string every time.
Drill: One Read, One Putt
Full Segment Practice
Practice the complete 10-segment process on every single practice putt. No shortcuts. What you practice is exactly what you will do under pressure.
Drill: 10-Ball Circle
Building Range and Confidence
Place 10 balls around the hole. Start at 3 feet. Once you make 10 in a row, move to 5 feet, then 10 feet. Practice on 1%, 2%, 3%, and 4% slopes when possible.
